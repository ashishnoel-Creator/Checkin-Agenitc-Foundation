# **1\. The Problem (Getting started with F\&B)**

## **1.1 Loyalty Is Universally Broken**

Every physical venue with repeat customers faces the same structural challenge: how do you build direct relationships that bring people back without depending on intermediaries or discounting your way to loyalty?

The tools available today are inadequate across verticals. Points-per-purchase systems, SMS blasts, birthday coupons, digital punch cards. Research shows over 50% of loyalty memberships become inactive. The reason is fundamental: these systems reward transactions, not behavior. They tell a customer “you’ve spent enough to earn a free coffee” but never answer “why should I come back to this specific place instead of the one next door?”

This loyalty infrastructure gap exists in restaurants, retail, gyms, salons, coworking spaces, and entertainment venues. The pain is universal; the current solutions are fragments.

## **1.2 The F\&B Aggregator Dependency Spiral**

In F\&B, the broken loyalty landscape compounds with a second structural problem: aggregator dependency.

India’s food services market is valued at **$85.19 billion (2025)**, projected to reach **$139.8 billion** by 2030\. Yet the venues generating this revenue are systematically losing control of their customer relationships.

**The delivery tax:** Venues pay **15–28%** commission on every delivery order, plus payment gateway fees, GST, platform fees (₹10–12/order), and packaging costs. Total effective cost can exceed **35%** of order value. The customer’s loyalty stays with the app. If a venue leaves Zomato, its delivery customers don’t follow.

**The dine-in trap:** Zomato Pay and Swiggy Dineout require participating venues to offer **15–40%** mandatory discounts plus **4–12%** commission per transaction. These discounts apply to any customer who pays through the app, including walk-ins who discovered the venue independently. 

A customer sits at your cafe because a friend recommended it, opens Zomato Pay, and the venue pays a 20% discount to an aggregator that contributed nothing to that visit.

**The data asymmetry:** Aggregators mask customer phone numbers behind relay systems. A venue can see ‘200 orders this week’ but can’t identify who ordered, whether they’re repeat customers, what else they eat, or when they’ll come back. The aggregator builds a rich customer graph across the ecosystem; the venue gets a report card.

## **1.3 The Failure of Current Solutions**

**Campaign-first tools:** Reelo **(28,000+ restaurants, ₹3.73 Cr revenue FY24, $1.27M total funding)** offers rule-based campaign automation: segment customers, blast WhatsApp messages, track points. This is a marketing tool. It tells venues what to say to whom but doesn’t change why customers return.

**POS-bundled loyalty:** Petpooja **(100,000+ restaurants, ₹910 Cr valuation)** includes basic loyalty as a checkbox feature on its POS. Points accumulation, cashback. Effective at capturing transaction data, but loyalty is an afterthought to billing.

**Discount-driven platforms:** Swiggy Dineout served 23.8 million covers in 2025, primarily through discounts. It recently partnered with Eat App for GroMax, outsourcing venue CRM rather than building it in-house. Dine-in intelligence infrastructure remains underdeveloped.

**The common failure:** None of these solutions create genuine engagement. They automate messages, accumulate points, or subsidize discounts. They don’t build the kind of experience that makes a customer choose a venue over its alternatives. This is a structural gap, not a feature gap.

**CheckIn is an OS for the Engagement Economy based on AI native architecture, that helps physical venues own their customer relationships through gamified experiences, a dual credit economy, and per-venue intelligent agents.** Starting with F\&B as the beachhead vertical, CheckIn’s architecture is designed to serve any venue where repeat visits drive revenue.

The system starts with human-in-the-loop (team designs, AI assists), moves to co-pilot mode (AI drafts, human approves), and graduates to autonomous operation within guardrails.

The AI agent suggests these automatically when it detects slow-moving items through POS data.

* **Smart ordering with POS integration:** Deep integration with POS systems (Petpooja, Posist) enables in-venue ordering with personalized menu suggestions. Connects the engagement layer directly to the transaction layer.

* **Table bookings & payments:** Reservations, payments infrastructure, and micro-transactions flow through CheckIn, enabling performance-based revenue on attributed footfall.

* **Plugin ecosystem:** Extensible platform for third-party tools (karaoke, event management, advanced feedback, kitchen display). Creates a marketplace opportunity and deepens platform integration.

## **2.2 For the Consumer**

**CheckIn launches a native consumer app alongside a lightweight web experience for seamless in-venue onboarding.** Diners scanning a QR code at a venue get instant access without interrupting the dining experience. The native app provides the full discovery and community experience.

* **Discovery & quests:** Personalized venue discovery. City-wide quests **(‘Koramangala Kebab Trail’)**, leaderboards, social challenges with friends.

* **Intelligent check-in:** QR scan triggers auto WiFi connection, instant venue follow, personalized welcome, and menu access. The check-in moment is the gateway to the entire experience, designed to be frictionless and immediately valuable.

* **Dual credit economy:** Earn **Venue Credits (VCs)** by completing quests at specific venues, redeemable only there. **Earn Global Credits (GCs)** for cross-venue activity, redeemable anywhere on CheckIn. Creates both venue lock-in and platform network effects.

* **Community:** Venue leaderboards, group quests, social proof, friend activity. Transforms visiting a venue from a transaction into a social game.

## **2.3 The Core Engagement Loop**

Diner visits venue → Scans QR / opens CheckIn → Auto-follows the venue → Sees personalized quests and menu recommendations → Completes quest actions → Earns Venue Credits → Redeems for rewards → Earns Global Credits for platform activity → GCs unlock discovery of new venues → Visits new venue → Cycle repeats.

**Check-in \= auto-follow.** The venue can reach the diner through notifications, feed updates, new quests, special events. The diner can unfollow to exit. No consent popups, no phone number sharing debates. The check-in IS the relationship.

# **5\. The Dual Credit Economy**

CheckIn’s credit system draws from Free-to-Play gaming economics: the Soft Currency vs. Hard Currency model that drives retention in mobile games.

## **5.1 Venue Credits (VCs) — The Lock-In Currency**

* Earned by completing quests and challenges at a specific venue

* Redeemable ONLY at that venue for goods, services, or experiences

* Non-transferable between venues; floating value set by venue (dynamic demand shaping)

* Dynamic decay: 60 days of inactivity triggers 10% balance degradation (prevents liability ballooning)

* Legal classification: loyalty points, exempt from VDA and PPI regulations (CBDT Notification 74/2022)

## **5.2 Global Credits (GCs) — The Liquidity Currency**

* Earned through platform-wide activity: cross-venue visits, referrals, brand-sponsored quests

* Redeemable at ANY CheckIn venue; fixed 1:1 INR peg (₹1 \= 1 GC)

* Strict backing: brand/marketing funds deposited before GCs are minted

* Creates the network effect: more venues \= more places to earn and redeem \= more valuable for every diner

## **5.3 The Swap Mechanism**

The GC→VC conversion is the key retention mechanism. A venue offers a minting bonus (‘Swap GCs for our VCs today → 50% bonus’). The diner swaps 1,000 GCs and receives 1,500 VCs. VCs generally cannot convert back to GCs (or incur \~50% penalty). The diner is now psychologically committed to returning. This is deliberate demand-shaping: venues can incentivize swaps on slow days, creating a dynamic pricing lever that doesn’t require discounting.

## **5.4 Regulatory Positioning**

Venue Credits are loyalty points with no monetary conversion — they cannot be transferred between users or cashed out. Global Credits are funded by brands/marketing budgets with 1:1 INR backing and strict issuance controls. Neither meets the RBI definition of a Prepaid Payment Instrument (minimum criteria: transferability, monetary value, redeemable for goods/services across unrelated merchants). The architecture is designed from the ground up for regulatory compliance, with legal review planned pre-launch.

# **6\. Agentic Architecture**

**CheckIn is not SaaS with AI bolted on. The platform is built agentic-first:** every component is designed for autonomous operation, with humans in the loop during early stages and progressively less intervention as the system learns.

## **6.1 Human-in-the-Loop → Co-Pilot → Autonomous**

| Phase | How It Works | What the Agent Does |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **HITL** | Founding team designs quests and campaigns manually; AI provides data-driven suggestions | Analyzes POS data, identifies patterns, suggests quest parameters and timing. Human decides. |
| **Co-Pilot** | AI drafts complete campaigns and quests; venue owner approves, tweaks, or rejects before execution | Generates full campaign plans: target audience, messaging, quest mechanics, reward structure. Human approves. |
| **Autonomous** | AI runs campaigns within venue-set guardrails (budget limits, discount caps, brand guidelines); human monitors exceptions | Full cycle: identifies opportunity → creates quest → targets diners → measures results → optimizes. Reports to owner. |

Every manual workflow during the HITL phase is documented in structured logs. These logs become the training data and playbooks for the AI agents as they progress through co-pilot to autonomous operation. The founding team’s hands-on work is a deliberate investment in the intelligence layer.

## **6.2 MCP-Powered Integration Layer**

**CheckIn uses the Model Context Protocol (MCP) as its integration backbone.** MCP allows agents to connect to data from any source — POS systems, payment gateways, review platforms, social feeds, inventory systems — through standardized servers. Each venue’s agent isn’t a chatbot; it’s an intelligent system that:

* **Reads** data from multiple sources (POS transactions, reviews, booking patterns, inventory levels)

* **Reasons** about what it observes (slow-moving inventory, declining Friday footfall, a regular who hasn’t visited in 3 weeks)

* **Acts** on its reasoning (creates an inventory-linked quest, designs a win-back campaign, adjusts reward parameters)

* **Learn** from outcomes (which quests had highest completion? What reward level drives repeat visits? Which messaging works for which diner segments?)

The MCP architecture means CheckIn’s agents can integrate with new data sources and verticals without rebuilding the core system. When CheckIn expands from F\&B to retail, the agent gains access to retail POS and inventory MCPs while the reasoning and engagement layer remains the same. This is the technical foundation for the multi-vertical vision.

**6.3 Self-Evolving SaaS**

"Self-evolving" means two things at CheckIn — and both matter.

**First**, the platform intelligence compounds with usage. More venues generate more behavioral data. More data creates smarter agents. Smarter agents deliver better results. Better results attract more venues. Concretely: an AI agent managing a Koramangala brewery learns that 'Friday happy hour quests with 2-for-1 mechanics drive 3x more completions than flat discount quests.' That learning propagates across the platform — a newly onboarded venue in Chennai automatically benefits from quest designs tested and optimized across hundreds of venues in Bengaluru.

**Second**, and more fundamentally, the development itself is agentic. Because CheckIn's architecture is AI-native from the ground up, the platform can ship new features rapidly, test them across different locations, gather usage feedback, and have agents build better applications or features based on what they observe. The product adapts to how venues and diners actually use it. An agent doesn't just optimize existing quests — it can identify that venues in a particular neighborhood need a feature that doesn't exist yet, prototype it, and deploy it for testing. This is not a roadmap driven by a product team's quarterly planning cycle; it's a platform that evolves continuously in response to real usage patterns.

This compounding loop — smarter agents, faster development, adaptive features — is the long-term moat. It cannot be replicated by a tool that enters the market later, because the learning requires the data, the data requires the venue density, and the venue density takes time to build.

**6.4 MCP-Native Interoperability**

Because CheckIn is built on the Model Context Protocol from Day 1, the platform is natively interoperable with any MCP-compatible system. This opens possibilities that go well beyond CheckIn's own interface.

A venue owner can connect their preferred LLM to CheckIn's MCP servers and query their customer data directly: "Show me my top 20 customers who haven't visited in 2 weeks." A diner can book a table at a CheckIn venue through any MCP-compatible assistant. Automated bots can monitor venue performance and send WhatsApp alerts when specific thresholds are hit — a drop in Friday footfall, a spike in negative reviews, a quest that's underperforming.

The implication is strategic: CheckIn doesn't need to build every interface. By exposing its engagement engine, credit economy, and venue intelligence through MCP, CheckIn becomes infrastructure that other tools and agents plug into. The more systems connect, the more valuable the platform becomes — and the harder it is to replace.

# **10\. Defensibility**

## **10.1 Compounding Intelligence Moat**

Every check-in, quest completion, and credit transaction feeds the platform’s intelligence layer. Once diners accumulate Venue Credits and Global Credits across multiple venues, switching costs become real. Credit balances, quest progress, and community standing represent invested value that doesn’t transfer to a competing platform.

## **10.3 Architectural Moat**

## **10.4 Network Effects**

The Global Credit economy creates cross-venue network effects: every new venue makes every existing venue more valuable for diners (more places to earn and redeem). This effect accelerates after reaching critical density in a city, which is why the neighborhood-cluster GTM strategy is essential.

| **Gamified engagement unvalidated in India F\&B** | Medium | Gamification is validated in adjacent categories (gaming, fitness, fintech). F\&B-specific validation is the core hypothesis of the seed stage. |

# **13\. The Thesis, Distilled**

Loyalty infrastructure for physical venues is broken across every vertical. Existing tools automate messages; they don’t create experiences. In F\&B, this problem compounds with aggregator dependency that strips venues of their customer relationships.

**CheckIn builds the engagement architecture that’s missing.** A per-venue AI agent that learns and grows the customer base. A gamified quest engine that drives behavior change, not just reward accumulation. A dual credit economy that creates real switching costs and cross-venue network effects. A platform built agentic-first, designed to compound intelligence with every venue and every interaction.

F\&B is the beachhead — the highest-frequency, highest-pain vertical where the architecture proves itself. The long-term vision is the universal engagement platform for every physical venue where repeat visits drive revenue: retail, wellness, live events, coworking, hospitality.

**The question is not whether venues need to own their customer relationships. They do. The question is not whether loyalty needs reinvention. It does.**
